Exploring Bullying Trends: Victoria (2006-2018)

Explore the changing patterns of bullying in Victoria from 2006 to 2018 through engaging Visualisations. Gain insights into how bullying incidents among students in years 7 to 9 vary across different areas, providing a clear snapshot of the trends during this period. This dataset originates from comprehensive, yearly statewide surveys named Attitudes to School. These surveys evaluate students' school experiences, specifically addressing the query "Have you experienced bullying at school recently?"

Figure 1: The data for the year 2016 indicates a wide range of bullying prevalence (measured as 'Portion Bullied') among students in years 7 to 9 across Local Government Areas (LGAs) in Victoria. The percentages range from as low as 4.31% in Southern Grampians to as high as 30.5% in Northern Grampians, illustrating significant disparities in bullying rates across regions.

Figure 2 and 3 Respectively: Analyzing bullying rates in Victoria's Local Government Areas (LGAs) from 2014 to 2016, we observe varied trends. Metropolitan Melbourne generally reports lower rates compared to the state average. Some areas, like Ararat and Greater Shepparton, saw significant increases, raising concerns. Conversely, Murrindindi and Yarra saw decreases, suggesting positive interventions. In 2016, Knox reported the highest bullying at 22.6%, while Stonnington had the lowest at 6% within metropolitan Melbourne. These variations emphasize the need for targeted interventions and ongoing monitoring to ensure student well-being and safety.

Bullying Trends by Gender

Figure 4: Analyzing bullying rates from 2006 to 2016 by gender reveals an interesting evolution. In 2006, males had a slightly higher bullying rate at 31.7% compared to females at 30.2%. Over the years, overall bullying rates decreased. A noteworthy shift occurred around 2010-2011, coinciding with the popularization of social media. Males, once higher at 2.2%, now have a 1.9% lower bullying rate than females. This suggests a shift in bullying dynamics, possibly due to the emergence of cyberbullying (see below: Figure 6) as a prevalent issue.

Cyberbullying Trends

Figure 5: It's evident that females consistently reported higher rates than males. Metropolitan areas showed higher cyberbullying rates, likely due to increased digital access. Surprisingly, cyberbullying rates decreased in 2018 for both LBOTE (Language background other than English) and non-LBOTE groups, possibly due to anti-bullying efforts and heightened awareness. The slight dip in cyberbullying rates among older students in 2018 could be attributed to improved digital literacy and increased awareness. These trends highlight the need for targeted interventions to combat cyberbullying effectively.

Bullying Rates and Student School Connection

Figure 6: Here, we can see an inverse correlation between the percentage of bullied students and the percentage of students feeling connected to the school. Higher rates of bullying are associated with lower levels of perceived connection to the school. This indicates that when students experience less bullying, they tend to feel safer and more connected to the school community, emphasizing the need for creating a supportive and inclusive environment to enhance student well-being.
